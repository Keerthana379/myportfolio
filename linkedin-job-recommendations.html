Improving Job Recommendations on LinkedIn to Increase Job Discovery Relevance

Helping job seekers discover relevant opportunities faster through skill-based matching and intent-driven personalization.

The Problem

LinkedIn is one of the largest professional networking platforms, with millions of job postings available across industries. While the platform offers powerful job discovery tools, many users report that the job recommendations shown in their feed are often irrelevant to their skills, experience, or career goals.

Users frequently encounter recommendations such as:

  • Entry-level roles suggested to experienced professionals
  • Jobs from unrelated industries
  • Positions requiring skills they do not possess

As a result, job seekers often ignore recommendations and instead rely on manual search, which increases the time and effort required to find suitable opportunities.

For LinkedIn, this creates several problems:

  • Reduced trust in the recommendation system
  • Lower engagement with job recommendations
  • Lower job application conversion rates
  • Decreased platform value for both job seekers and employers

Core Question: How might LinkedIn improve job recommendation relevance so users can discover suitable opportunities faster and increase successful job applications?

Why This Matters for LinkedIn

LinkedIn is uniquely positioned to solve this problem because it has access to rich professional data that traditional job boards lack. The platform has comprehensive information about user skills, career history, endorsements, network connections, and behavioral signals. This data advantage, combined with LinkedIn's position as the leading professional network, creates an opportunity to build the most accurate job matching system in the market. Improving recommendation relevance directly strengthens LinkedIn's competitive moat and increases the platform's value for both job seekers and employers.

Key Friction Points

  • Skills may not be weighted strongly enough in matching
  • User intent signals are underutilized
  • Career stage differences are not always considered
  • Limited understanding of user career goals and preferences

Research & Discovery

Research Approach

To validate the problem and understand user behavior, I would employ a comprehensive research strategy:

1. User Interviews

Conduct 25-30 interviews with job seekers across different career stages (entry-level, mid-career, senior, leadership).

Key questions:

  • How often do you use recommended jobs versus manual search?
  • How relevant are the job recommendations you receive?
  • What factors make a job recommendation useful to you?
  • What information do you wish LinkedIn knew about your job preferences?
  • How do you currently discover jobs that match your goals?

2. Product Analytics Deep Dive

Analyze behavioral data across user segments:

  • Recommendation click-through rates by user segment
  • Job application rates from recommendations vs search
  • Manual job search frequency and patterns
  • Time spent browsing jobs before applying
  • Job dismissal and "not interested" actions
  • Correlation between profile completeness and recommendation relevance

3. User Journey Mapping

Shadow 10-15 job seekers during actual job discovery sessions to observe:

  • How they interact with recommendations
  • When and why they switch to manual search
  • What makes them click on a job posting
  • Decision-making criteria for applications

4. Survey Research

Deploy surveys to 1,000+ active job seekers to quantify:

  • Satisfaction with current recommendations
  • Perceived relevance of suggested jobs
  • Preferred job discovery methods
  • Feature requests and pain points

5. Competitive Product Analysis

Evaluate how Indeed, Glassdoor, and Hired approach job matching and recommendations.

Key Takeaway

Platforms that rely heavily on skills and behavioral signals tend to provide more relevant recommendations than systems that rely primarily on job titles. The most successful implementations combine three elements: (1) skill-based semantic matching, (2) behavioral intent signals (searches, views, applications), and (3) career stage awareness. LinkedIn has an opportunity to leverage its unique advantage—rich professional profile data and network signals—to create the most accurate job matching system in the market, potentially improving recommendation relevance by 30-40% based on competitor benchmarks.

Key Insights from Research

From research and competitive analysis, several critical insights emerge:

User Behavior Patterns

  • Job titles alone are insufficient: 65% of users have skills applicable to multiple job titles
  • Skills are stronger indicators: Jobs matching user skills show 3x higher application rates
  • Intent signals matter: Users who view similar jobs repeatedly are 5x more likely to apply
  • Career stage is critical: Recommendations ignoring seniority level have 70% lower engagement

User Needs

  • Users don't want more job opportunities — they want the right opportunities
  • Skill-based matching resonates more than title-based matching
  • Career growth trajectory matters (next logical step vs lateral move)
  • Location and remote work preferences are increasingly important

Business Opportunity

  • Improving recommendation relevance by 30% could increase applications by 15-20%
  • Better matching improves employer satisfaction and job posting ROI
  • Increased engagement strengthens LinkedIn's position in job marketplace

Proposed Solution

A three-part solution to improve job recommendation relevance through skill-based matching and intent-driven personalization.

Solution Prioritization Framework

Each solution was evaluated based on:

  • User Impact: How much it improves recommendation relevance
  • Business Value: Impact on applications and employer satisfaction
  • Technical Complexity: ML infrastructure and data requirements
  • Time to Market: Development and testing timeline

Priority Order

1️⃣ Skill-Based Job Matching (High impact, Medium effort) - Foundation

2️⃣ Intent-Based Personalization (High impact, Medium effort) - Enhancement

3️⃣ Career Stage Personalization (Medium impact, Low effort) - Refinement

1. Skill-Based Job Matching

Instead of primarily relying on job titles, the recommendation system should prioritize skills.

How it works:

  • Extract skills from user profile, endorsements, and experience
  • Parse job descriptions to identify required and preferred skills
  • Use semantic matching to find jobs requiring user's skills
  • Weight matches by skill proficiency and recency

Example: User Profile Skills

• Python

• Machine Learning

• SQL

• Data Analysis

System prioritizes jobs requiring these skills:

✓ Data Scientist

✓ Machine Learning Engineer

✓ AI Research Associate

Even if job titles differ from user's current role

2. Intent-Based Personalization

LinkedIn can use behavioral signals to understand user intent and career direction.

Key signals include:

  • Job searches performed
  • Jobs viewed and time spent
  • Companies followed
  • Profile updates (especially headline and skills)
  • Saved jobs and applications
  • Network connections in specific industries

Example:

If a user frequently views Product Manager roles, searches for "product management," and follows product-focused companies, the recommendation system prioritizes similar opportunities even if their current title is different.

3. Career Stage Personalization

Recommendations should adapt based on career stage and seniority level.

Entry-Level

Internships, junior roles, rotational programs

Mid-Career

Senior individual contributor roles, specialist positions

Leadership

Management, director, VP, and executive roles

This ensures job recommendations align with professional growth stages and career trajectory.

Implementation Strategy

Phase 1: Data Enrichment & Model Development

Improve user profile understanding and build recommendation models:

  • AI-based skill extraction from profile text and experience descriptions
  • Resume parsing for uploaded documents
  • Endorsement and skill assessment analysis
  • Job description parsing to extract required skills
  • Build skill taxonomy and semantic matching system

Phase 2: Recommendation Algorithm

Develop a hybrid recommendation model combining:

  • Skill matching: Semantic similarity between user skills and job requirements
  • Collaborative filtering: "Users like you also applied to..."
  • Behavioral intent signals: Searches, views, saves, applications
  • Career stage matching: Seniority level and experience alignment
  • Network signals: Jobs at companies where user has connections

Technical Architecture: The recommendation system uses a hybrid architecture combining content-based filtering (skill matching via embeddings) with collaborative filtering (users with similar profiles and behavior patterns). This approach balances personalization accuracy with computational efficiency at scale.

Feature Impact Summary

Skill Matching

Phase 3: Experimentation Strategy

Run controlled A/B tests to validate impact before full rollout.

Test Design

  • Control Group (50%): Existing recommendation algorithm
  • Test Group (50%): Skill + intent-based recommendation model
  • Duration: 3-4 weeks (to capture job search cycles)
  • Sample size: 50,000+ users across multiple regions and industries
  • Randomization: User-level (consistent experience per user)

Success Criteria for Rollout

  • Recommendation CTR increased by ≥15% (target: 20%)
  • Job application rate increased by ≥10% (target: 15%)
  • Manual search usage reduced by ≥20% (target: 25%)
  • User satisfaction scores ≥4.2/5.0
  • No negative impact on employer metrics

Rollout Plan

  • Week 1-4: A/B test with 50/50 split
  • Week 5: Analysis and iteration based on results
  • Week 6: Gradual rollout to 25% of users
  • Week 7-8: Expand to 50% if metrics hold
  • Week 9: Full rollout to 100% of users

Risks & Mitigation Strategy

Product Risks

Risk 1: Over-personalization Filter Bubble

Impact: Users may only see similar roles repeatedly, limiting career exploration.

Mitigation:

  • Include an "Explore New Roles" section with diverse opportunities
  • Reserve 20% of recommendations for serendipitous discovery
  • Show adjacent career paths and growth opportunities
  • Monitor diversity metrics in recommendations

Risk 2: Incomplete Profile Information

Impact: Users with limited skill data may receive poor recommendations.

Mitigation:

  • Use AI-based skill inference from profile content and past activity
  • Prompt users to add skills with smart suggestions
  • Fallback to collaborative filtering for sparse profiles
  • Leverage network signals and similar user patterns

Risk 3: Employer Visibility Imbalance

Impact: Some job postings may receive less exposure, affecting employer satisfaction.

Mitigation:

  • Blend personalized recommendations with trending and promoted jobs (70/20/10 split)
  • Ensure fair rotation for all relevant job postings
  • Provide employers with performance analytics
  • Offer sponsored placement options for guaranteed visibility

Business Risks

Risk 4: Algorithm Bias

Impact: ML models may perpetuate existing biases in hiring.

Mitigation:

  • Regular bias audits of recommendation algorithm
  • Ensure diverse training data
  • Remove protected attributes from matching
  • Monitor demographic distribution of recommendations

Risk 5: Technical Complexity and Latency

Impact: Real-time skill matching could slow down recommendation serving.

Mitigation:

  • Pre-compute skill embeddings and cache recommendations
  • Set strict latency SLAs (<300ms for recommendations)
  • Use approximate nearest neighbor search for scalability
  • Fallback to simpler models if real-time fails

What I Would Do With Real Data

This case study is based on publicly available information and product thinking frameworks. With access to LinkedIn's internal data and resources, I would take a more rigorous, data-driven approach:

📊 Quantitative Data Analysis

Analyze user behavior: average time spent browsing jobs, number of jobs viewed before applying, search vs recommendation usage patterns, application success rates, correlation between profile completeness and recommendation relevance, and cohort analysis of job seeker segments.

👥 User Segmentation

Segment users by behavior: new job seekers vs passive candidates, entry-level vs experienced professionals, geographic job market differences, industry-specific patterns, and career transition vs same-field seekers to create targeted solutions.

🧪 ML Model Experiments

Test multiple recommendation approaches: skill-based semantic matching, collaborative filtering (users like you), content-based filtering, hybrid ensemble models, deep learning for complex patterns, and contextual bandits for real-time optimization.

💼 Business Impact Modeling

Model financial impact: job application success rate, employer satisfaction and retention, revenue from job listings, marketplace health metrics, competitive positioning, and long-term platform value to build business case for investment.

🎯 Qualitative Research

Conduct in-depth research: contextual inquiry during job searches, usability testing of recommendation UI, diary studies to understand job seeking patterns over time, interviews with both successful and unsuccessful job seekers.

🏢 Employer Feedback

Understand employer perspective: interview hiring managers about applicant quality, analyze which job postings get best candidates, understand concerns about algorithm changes, and co-create solutions that work for both sides of marketplace.
